Course Topics

Programming in both Python and Java, with Windows and Linux operating systems. Variables and expressions, I/O, functions, conditionals, iteration, and collections. Introduction to complex data types and object-oriented programming. Also, some exposure to visual programming environments.

Introductory material on computer architecture, program translation, operating systems, and software engineering theory.

To be elaborated on throughout the semester...


Course Description

Learn how to program a computer with two popular languages, Python and Java. Create animations, programs that talk to Google, and programs that manipulate images and sound. Learn to use the Linux operating system. Learn basic computer science concepts, like how program code is translated into the electronic currents in a computer. And learn how to use the newest web technologies like wikis, blogs, newsreaders, and even a bit of del.icio.us. Most importantly, learn how to problem-solve.


Course Format

Learn-by-Doing! The course is a lecture/lab format, with lecture, discussion, and computer labs each day.

Participation in class is essential. Attendance and participation is crucial. How-to-program knowledge is stored in our brains and finger-tips. Missing even one class can put you way behind (and slow down the class). And 10% of your grade is based on mini-labs completed in class. These assignments cannot be made up (without a doctor's note).


Prerequisites

No programming experience is required, though some in the class will have some. The pace will be faster than CS 107, which is primarily for non-majors, but quite doable even for those with no programming experience. It will be fun and hands-on!


Why take this course?

After this course, you will know more about programming a computer than 99.9% of humans on this earth. Even if you end up going into business or another major, taking a few computer science courses can be the difference between getting that job or not.

You'll learn how to think better, and how to problem solve. Think of computer programming as applied logic. You get to work on your logic and math skills, but in the context of building things.

Learn what computer science is, and what exciting things you can do with it.

First course in the CS Major.

Covers the Core B Math requirement at USF.

Academic Dishonesty

Students are required to follow the University's Honor Code: "As a Jesuit institution committed to cura personalis- the care and education of the whole person- USF has an obligation to embody and foster the values of honesty and integrity. USF upholds the standards of honesty and integrity from all members of the academic community. All students are expected to know and adhere to the University’s Honor Code. " You can find the full text of the code online at https://myusf.usfca.edu/academic-integrity.

ALL assignments are to be completed individually unless specified, in writing, on the assignment. Academic dishonesty will NOT be tolerated. This is your warning! Students are encouraged to meet with me if they have questions regarding assignments or this policy. Students caught cheating will face severe penalty.


Students may:

Receive help from the professor and the TA

Discuss the requirements of the assignments, the meaning of programs, or high-level algorithms with other students or outside sources. If you have any doubt with respect to what is acceptable to discuss, speak with the professor first


Students may not:

Look at another student's code

Receive unapproved help from an outsie source including a tutor or family member

Submit code which has, in whole or in part, been copied from any other source (including another student, a web page, or another text)

Submit solutions to problems which have, in whole or in part, been copied from any other source (including another student, a web page, or another text)


Cheating Penalties

Students caught violating the academic honesty policy will face severe penalty. A first offense will result in a zero on the assignment and a report to the Dean's office. A second offense will result in the student being spanked and failing the course.
